Theorem 2.1.22.

The following properties hold for vectors \(\mathbf{u}\text{,}\) \(\mathbf{v}\) and \({\bf w}\) in \(\R^n\) and scalars \(k\) and \(p\) in \(\R\text{.}\)
  1. Commutative Property of Addition
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{u} \end{equation*}
  2. Associative Property of Addition
    \begin{equation*} (\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v})+{\bf w}=\mathbf{u}+(\mathbf{v}+{\bf w}) \end{equation*}
  3. Existence of Additive Identity: There exists a vector \(\mathbf{0}\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{u}+\mathbf{0}=\mathbf{u} \end{equation*}
  4. Existence of Additive Inverse: For every vector \(\mathbf{u}\text{,}\) there exists a vector \(-\mathbf{u}\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{u}+(-\mathbf{u})=\mathbf{0} \end{equation*}
  5. Distributive Property over Vector Addition
    \begin{equation*} k(\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v})=k\mathbf{u}+k\mathbf{v} \end{equation*}
  6. Distributive Property over Scalar Addition
    \begin{equation*} (k+p)\mathbf{u}=k\mathbf{u}+p\mathbf{u} \end{equation*}
  7. Associative Property for Scalar Multiplication
    \begin{equation*} k(p\mathbf{u})=(kp)\mathbf{u} \end{equation*}
  8. Multiplication by 1
    \begin{equation*} 1\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{u} \end{equation*}

Proof.

We will prove Item 4 and Item 5. Proofs of the remaining properties are left to the reader.
For any vector \(\mathbf{u}=\begin{bmatrix} u_1\\ u_2\\ \vdots\\ u_n \end{bmatrix}\) in \(\R^n\text{,}\) let
\begin{equation*} -\mathbf{u}=(-1)\mathbf{u}=\begin{bmatrix} -u_1\\ -u_2\\ \vdots\\ -u_n \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
Then \(\mathbf{u}+(-\mathbf{u})=\mathbf{0}\text{.}\)
Now to prove Item 5:
\begin{equation*} k(\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v})=k\left(\begin{bmatrix} u_1\\ u_2\\ \vdots\\ u_n \end{bmatrix}+\begin{bmatrix} v_1\\ v_2\\ \vdots\\ v_n \end{bmatrix}\right)=k\begin{bmatrix} u_1+v_1\\ u_2+v_2\\ \vdots\\ u_n+v_n \end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix} k(u_1+v_1)\\ k(u_2+v_2)\\ \vdots\\ k(u_n+v_n) \end{bmatrix}= \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} =\begin{bmatrix} ku_1+kv_1\\ ku_2+kv_2\\ \vdots\\ ku_n+kv_n \end{bmatrix} =\begin{bmatrix} ku_1\\ ku_2\\ \vdots\\ ku_n \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} kv_1\\ kv_2\\ \vdots\\ kv_n \end{bmatrix} =k\begin{bmatrix}u_1\\ u_2\\ \vdots\\ u_n \end{bmatrix}+k\begin{bmatrix} v_1\\ v_2\\ \vdots\\ v_n \end{bmatrix} =k\mathbf{u}+k\mathbf{v} \end{equation*}
