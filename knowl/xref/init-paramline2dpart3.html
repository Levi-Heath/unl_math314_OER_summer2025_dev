Exploration 1.1.4.

Let’s return to parametric equations from the previous problem.
\begin{align*} x\amp=2t-1 \\ y\amp=-3t+6 \end{align*}
Consider the table of values we constructed in Exploration 1.1.2.
\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{|c|c|c|} \hline t\amp x \amp y\\ \hline 0 \amp -1 \amp 6\\ \hline 1 \amp 1 \amp 3 \\ \hline 2 \amp 3\amp 0\\ \hline 3 \amp 5\amp -3\\ \hline \end{array} \end{equation*}
Observe that every time \(t\) increases by \(1\text{,}\) \(x\) increases by \(2\) and \(y\) decreases by \(3\text{.}\) An increase by \(2\) in the \(x\)-coordinate combined with a decrease of \(3\) in the \(y\)-coordinate corresponds to the coefficients \(2\) and \(-3\) of \(t\) in the parametric equations. Our findings are in agreement with the fact that the slope of the line is \(-\frac{3}{2}\text{.}\)
We will capture the ``rise" and ``run" aspect of the line by using vector \(\mathbf{v}=\begin{bmatrix}2\\-3\end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Sketching \(\mathbf{v}\) together with the line, we find that the vector is parallel to the line.
Vector \(\mathbf{v}\) is called a direction vector. Observe that the components of \(\mathbf{v}\) are the coefficients of \(t\) in the parametric equations.
\begin{align*} x\amp={\color{red}2}t-1 \\ y\amp={\color{red}-3}t+6 \end{align*}
Next, let’s turn our attention to constants \(-1\) and \(6\text{.}\) They are the values of \(x\) and \(y\) when \(t=0\text{,}\) and correspond to the point \((-1, 6)\text{.}\)
Recall that in Exploration 1.1.2 we said that the given parametric equations describe the position of a ladybug crawling in the coordinate plane. If we are concerned with the position of the ladybug at time \(t\text{,}\) the point \((-1, 6)\) is very important - this is where the bug is located when \(t=0\text{.}\) But, if we are using parametric equations simply to describe the line \(y=-\frac{3}{2}x+\frac{9}{2}\text{,}\) without regard for when the bug is located at each point, then the point \((-1, 6)\) is not any more special than any other point on the line. In fact, we can use any other point on this line to find another set of parametric equations that describe the same line. In [cross-reference to target(s) "prob-paramnotunique" missing or not unique] you will be asked to show that equations
\begin{align*} x\amp=2t+1 \\ y\amp=-3t+3 \end{align*}
describe the same line. Thus, parametric representations are not unique.
